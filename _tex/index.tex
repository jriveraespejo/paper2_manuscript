\section{Introduction}\label{sec-introduction}

In contemporary contexts, Comparative Judgment (CJ), as stated by
Thurstone (1927), is primarily associated with the method of
\emph{Dichotomous} Comparative Judgment (DCJ). Within this approach, a
panel of judges evaluates the relative manifestation of a particular
trait or quality within a pair of stimuli. The result of this evaluation
produces a dichotomous value, indicating which stimulus in the pair has
a higher degree of the trait or quality (Pollitt 2012a, 2012b). It has
been shown that DCJ produces reliable and valid measures of the assessed
traits and qualities (Lesterhuis 2018; van Daal 2020). Consequently, DCJ
have been successfully employed to assess competences and traits in
various domains, including education (Bartholomew et al. 2018;
Lesterhuis 2018; Pollitt 2012b; van Daal 2020; Bartholomew and Williams
2020), linguistics (Boonen, Kloots, and Gillis 2020), and psychology
{(citation needed)}. In summary, DCJ presents itself as an alternative
and efficient approach to measurement and evaluation that is reliable
and valid across diverse domains.
